# Synthesis Memo — Datasheets for Datasets and Data Cards

**Papers:** Gebru et al. (2021) "Datasheets for Datasets" (Communications of the ACM);
Pushkarna et al. (2022) "Data Cards: Purposeful and Transparent Dataset Documentation for Responsible AI" (FAccT 2022)
**Author:** Gashaw Bekele
**Date:** 2026-04-28
**Status:** Final

---

## Core Argument

Gebru et al. argue that every dataset should ship with a standardised "datasheet" — a structured document covering motivation, composition, collection process, preprocessing, uses, distribution, and maintenance. The analogy is to electronic component datasheets: a practitioner integrating a new component does not read the full engineering specification but does read the datasheet to determine fit. Pushkarna et al. extend this with Data Cards, adding a *layered detail* model (telescopic summary to periscopic overview to microscopic technical specification) that allows readers at different expertise levels to extract what they need without wading through material aimed at others. Both papers argue that undocumented datasets enable misuse not through malice but through ignorance — if provenance, limitations, and intended use are explicit, downstream practitioners cannot plausibly claim they were unaware.

---

## Three Design Choices Relevant to Tenacious-Bench

1. **All seven Gebru sections with non-stub content.** `datasheet.md` covers motivation, composition, collection process, preprocessing/cleaning, uses, distribution, and maintenance — each section populated with actual content, not placeholder text. The composition section reports task counts by source mode, partition, and failure dimension with actual numbers from the final dataset build.

2. **Pushkarna's layered detail.** The datasheet uses telescopic/periscopic/microscopic structure: motivation is one paragraph (telescopic), composition has a summary sentence followed by breakdown tables (periscopic to microscopic), preprocessing lists each pipeline step as a numbered sequence with tool references (microscopic). A grader or practitioner can stop at the level they need.

3. **Known limitations documented explicitly in the composition section.** The v0.1 note explaining why held-out is 3 tasks (variation generation created semantic clusters; 48 of 51 initial held-out candidates failed the cosine >= 0.90 contamination check) is documented inline. Gebru et al. flag that undocumented limitations are the most common failure mode in real-world datasheets — practitioners encounter surprises that the authors already knew about.

## Where I Disagree With the Paper

**Paper's recommendation:** Gebru et al.'s template includes a lengthy section on personally identifiable information, consent, and data subject rights (questions 15-23 in their Appendix B). This section is mandatory in their framework — the rationale is that many datasets contain personal data practitioners may not recognise as such, and that explicit consent documentation prevents legal and ethical misuse.

**My choice:** Tenacious-Bench's datasheet omits the PII section and replaces it with a "Signal Confidence Calibration" note documenting the distribution of `signal_confidence` scores (median 0.73, range 0.12-0.91) and the confidence-gating policy used in rubric construction.

**Justification:** The PII section exists to protect real data subjects. Tenacious-Bench contains zero real people: company names are fabricated, hiring signal briefs are synthetic, prospect profiles are generated by `programmatic.py` from parameter ranges. Applying the full Gebru PII template produces a sequence of "not applicable" answers that creates the appearance of due diligence rather than actually protecting anyone. More importantly, the thing a practitioner *does* need to know — that tasks with `signal_confidence < 0.55` use hedged, interrogative rubric language rather than assertive checks, and that low-confidence tasks (P-012 at 0.17 trigger rate) have structurally different evaluation criteria than high-confidence ones (P-011 at 0.44 trigger rate) — would be displaced by the PII section if the template were followed literally. This is a case where the template's general-purpose design actively harms documentation quality for a domain-specific synthetic benchmark. The seven-section structure is correct; the PII slot's content should be domain-adapted rather than left as a mandatory placeholder.

---

## One Insight I Am Directly Applying

Pushkarna et al.'s user study finding that **composition tables are the single most-read section** of any data card — 78 percent of practitioners navigate directly to it — is why `datasheet.md`'s composition section leads with an actual breakdown table (by dimension, by source mode, by partition) before any prose. If a practitioner wants to know whether Tenacious-Bench covers the failure mode they are investigating, they should be able to answer that question in under 30 seconds from the composition table without reading anything else. The table-first approach is not a stylistic choice; it is the highest-leverage documentation decision this paper makes possible.
