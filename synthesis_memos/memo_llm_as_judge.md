# Synthesis Memo — LLM-as-a-Judge

**Paper:** Zheng et al. (2023) "Judging LLM-as-a-Judge with MT-Bench and Chatbot Arena" (NeurIPS 2023);
supplemented by Gu et al. (2024) "A Survey on LLM-as-a-Judge"
**Author:** Gashaw Bekele
**Date:** 2026-04-28
**Status:** Final

---

## Core Argument

Zheng et al. demonstrate that a strong LLM judge (GPT-4) achieves over 80 percent agreement with human raters on multi-turn dialogue quality, making automated evaluation feasible at scale. They introduce MT-Bench (80 challenging multi-turn questions) and Chatbot Arena (crowdsourced pairwise comparisons) as complementary evaluation axes. Gu et al. extend this across 18 benchmarks, confirming reliability for relative ranking but documenting three systematic failure modes: **position bias** (preferring the first response in pairwise comparisons), **verbosity bias** (preferring longer responses regardless of quality), and **self-enhancement bias** (a model judges tasks it generated more leniently than a different family would).

---

## Three Design Choices Relevant to Tenacious-Bench

1. **Pointwise over pairwise scoring.** Zheng et al. show pairwise is more discriminative, but pairwise requires a reference response. Tenacious-Bench tasks have no ground-truth output — we score against a rubric, not a reference email. Pointwise 1-5 is the only viable approach. Applied in `scoring_evaluator.py::llm_judge_score()`.

2. **Rubric-anchored prompts over holistic quality.** Gu et al. find rubric-anchored prompts reduce inter-rater variance by roughly 30 percent versus holistic quality prompts. The Style Guide v2 five-marker framework (Direct, Grounded, Honest, Professional, Non-condescending) with explicit 1/3/5 anchor descriptions in `TONE_JUDGE_PROMPT` is a direct application of this finding.

3. **Temperature zero for reproducibility.** Zheng et al. use temperature 0 for the judge and note that stochastic judges produce inter-run variance that obscures real signal. Tenacious-Bench judge calls should pin to temperature 0 in production (currently using model default — a v0.2 hardening item).

---

## Where I Disagree With the Paper

**Paper's recommendation:** Zheng et al. (Section 4.2) recommend using a *single* powerful judge — specifically GPT-4 — for all evaluations, citing consistency as the primary rationale. They do not rotate judges across task types or generating models.

**My choice:** Tenacious-Bench rotates judge families based on the synthesis model that generated each task. Tasks generated by Claude/Anthropic-class models are judged by an OpenRouter dev-tier model (Qwen); tasks generated by Qwen are judged by Claude. Enforced in `generation_scripts/judge_filter.py::pick_judge_model()`.

**Justification:** The single-judge recommendation assumes the judge and data-generating process are independent. In Tenacious-Bench they are not: if the same Claude model that writes seed tasks also evaluates whether those tasks meet quality thresholds, self-enhancement bias creates a self-referential loop. Gu et al. (2024) quantify self-enhancement bias at 8-15 percentage points across model families — large enough to inflate pass rates on Claude-generated tasks and degrade the downstream training signal. The rotation policy trades consistency for independence, which is the correct exchange when the dataset feeds a fine-tuning pipeline. Concrete evidence: after correcting the OpenRouter routing bug that was initially sending all judge calls to Claude regardless of synthesis model, the first properly-rotated run passed 185/189 tasks. The 4 failures were all on Claude-generated tasks that Qwen flagged for tone violations the generating model had not penalised. This is precisely the self-enhancement pattern the rotation is designed to catch. A single-judge setup would have passed all 189 and shipped those violations into training data.

---

## One Insight I Am Directly Applying

Gu et al.'s finding that rubric-anchored prompts reduce inter-rater variance by ~30 percent is the single most operationally useful result for this project. It is why `TONE_JUDGE_PROMPT` in `scoring_evaluator.py` provides per-marker 1/3/5 anchor descriptions rather than asking "how good is this email overall?" The Style Guide v2 supplied the marker definitions; this paper justified the investment in explicit anchoring over holistic prompting.
